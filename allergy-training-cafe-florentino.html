01

Why this matters

An allergen we can't see can still send a guest to hospital. In hospitality, taking allergies seriously isn't optional — it's the law, and it can be the difference between a normal shift and a tragedy.

1 in 10
babies in Australia are affected by food allergy
1 in 50
adults live with a food allergy — it rarely "goes away" once it starts in adulthood
170+
different foods are known to have triggered allergic reactions

Australia has some of the highest food-allergy rates in the world. Hospital admissions for severe reactions have risen steadily over the last 30 years. For a person with a true allergy, a trace amount — a shared fryer, a wiped-not-washed knife, a garnish — can be enough.

The legal bit — Australian law Under the Food Standards Code (Standard 1.2.3) and the Plain English Allergen Labelling (PEAL) rules in force since Feb 2024, food that doesn't carry a label — like a dish served in a restaurant — must still have accurate allergen information available, and we must provide it to a guest on request. "I'm not sure" is never an acceptable answer to a guest with an allergy.
02

Allergy vs Intolerance

These two words get used interchangeably by guests — but for us they mean very different levels of risk. Knowing the difference changes how seriously we treat an order.

Food allergy

The immune system mistakes a harmless food protein for a threat and overreacts. Reactions are fast (minutes to ~2 hours), can involve skin, gut, breathing and heart, and can escalate to anaphylaxis — a life-threatening emergency. Even a tiny trace can trigger it. It is not about quantity.

Food intolerance

The body has trouble digesting or processing a food — it does not involve the immune system. It causes discomfort (bloating, gut upset, headaches) but is not life-threatening and won't cause anaphylaxis. It's usually dose-dependent: a small amount may be fine, a large amount won't be.

The third category — autoimmune Coeliac disease is neither a classic allergy nor a simple intolerance — it's an autoimmune condition where gluten damages the gut lining. It affects about 1 in 70 Australians. It won't cause anaphylaxis, but the damage is real and serious, and even tiny cross-contamination matters. Treat a coeliac "gluten free" request with the same strictness as an allergy.
Golden rule If you're ever unsure whether a guest means "allergy" or "preference" — ask, and treat it as an allergy until you know otherwise. Over-caution costs nothing. Under-caution can cost a life.
03

The confusing pairs

Most allergen mistakes in a kitchen come from words that sound similar but mean different things. Here are the ones that trip people up most.

Dairy allergy VS Lactose intolerance

Both involve dairy — but one is dangerous and one is digestive. Don't mix them up.

Dairy allergy (the protein)

An immune reaction to milk proteins (casein & whey). Can cause anaphylaxis. Must avoid all dairy — including butter, cream, cheese, yoghurt, milk powder, and hidden traces. "Lactose-free milk" is NOT safe for them — it still has the proteins.

Lactose intolerance (the sugar)

Can't digest lactose, the sugar in milk (missing the enzyme lactase). Causes bloating & gut upset, not anaphylaxis. Often handles small amounts, hard cheeses, or lactose-free products fine. About comfort, not safety.
On the floor: If a guest says "dairy allergy," lock down every trace of milk protein. If they say "lactose intolerant," lactose-free / plant milk options solve it — it's a comfort request, not an emergency.

"Shellfish": Crustaceans VS Molluscs

"Shellfish" covers two separate families — crustaceans and molluscs. Australian law (PEAL) lists them separately, and someone can be allergic to one and not the other. But our rule is simpler and safer:

Our rule When a guest says "no shellfish," it means BOTH families — no crustaceans and no molluscs. We never narrow it down or assume they only mean prawns. We're not here to tell a guest they've used the wrong word — we're here to keep them safe and give them the best possible experience.

Shellfish also hides in stocks, fish sauce, XO sauce, laksa, oyster sauce and Asian soups & dressings — check the base, not just the protein on the plate. "No shellfish" still rules these out.

Finned fish VS Shellfish

Fish (finned)

Salmon, tuna, snapper, barramundi, anchovy, cod. Hides in Worcestershire sauce, Caesar dressing, fish sauce, stocks, and bouillabaisse.

Different allergen entirely

Being allergic to fish does not mean allergic to shellfish, and vice versa. They are unrelated allergen groups — always confirm exactly which.
Watch-out: Anchovy is the classic hidden fish — it lurks in Caesar dressing, puttanesca, and many pizza/pasta bases. Here: anchovy is hiding in the salsa verde, the Livornese sauce on the market fish, and the warm vinaigrette on the fagioli. Tuna also sits inside the vitello tonnato mayonnaise — a veal dish that is not fish-free.

Wheat allergy VS Gluten / Coeliac

Wheat allergy

An immune (IgE) allergy to wheat. Can cause anaphylaxis. Must avoid wheat — but may be fine with other gluten grains depending on the person.

Coeliac / gluten

Coeliac is autoimmune (~1 in 70 Australians): gluten (in wheat, rye, barley, and gluten-containing oats) damages the gut. No anaphylaxis, but strict avoidance and no cross-contamination required. "Gluten sensitivity" is milder again.
On the floor: A "gluten free" request can be a serious coeliac (treat strictly — separate prep, clean surfaces) or a lifestyle choice. Always ask "Is this an allergy or coeliac, or a preference?" so the kitchen knows how careful to be. Here: all our fresh pasta (ravioli doppio, smoked mussels tagliatelle, short rib saccottini) is wheat-based, plus the suppli, the cotoletta crumb, the gnocchi fritti cracker under the bresaola, the focaccia with the duck liver parfait, the croutons in the insalata, and the semolina cracker on the cheese plate — none are gluten-free.

Peanuts VS Tree nuts

Peanut (a legume!)

Botanically a legume — it grows underground, like peas and lentils, not on a tree. One of the top triggers of fatal anaphylaxis.

Tree nuts (each is separate)

AlmondCashewWalnutHazelnutPistachioMacadamiaPecanBrazil nutPine nut
On the floor: Peanut allergy ≠ tree-nut allergy — they're unrelated, though many people avoid both to be safe. Under PEAL each tree nut must be named individually, so "nut free" is too vague — ask which nuts.

The allergens, most to least common

These are the foods behind the vast majority of allergic reactions in Australia. Each card shows what the allergen is, the Australian prevalence where it's known, the foods it covers, where it likes to hide — and real examples from our current Florentino menu.

Note: exact ranking shifts with age — dairy and egg dominate in young children, while shellfish allergy tends to appear later and last for life. The order below reflects overall Australian frequency.

Where the percentages come from The most reliable Australian figures come from infant and childhood studies (notably the HealthNuts study, Melbourne). Rates change a lot with age: dairy, egg, soy and wheat allergies are often outgrown, while peanut, tree nut, seafood and sesame allergies are more often lifelong. So among our adult diners, those four are the ones we'll meet most.

Hidden allergens on our menu — the ones that catch people out

The allergen isn't always in the obvious ingredient. These are the Florentino traps to know cold — and always confirm against the Allergy Matrix.

Vitello tonnato — the creamy sauce is built on tuna and egg. A veal dish that is not fish-free and not egg-free.
Salsa verde, Livornese sauce (market fish) & fagioli vinaigrette — all contain anchovy. Hidden fish in a sauce, a fish dish, and a side of beans.
Wood-fired eggplant — finished with roasted pine nuts. A vegetarian main that is not tree-nut-free.
Mostarda alla romana — contains almonds and pine nuts. A condiment that can be served alongside meats — easy to miss.
Panna cotta — set with gelatine, which is pork-derived. Not halal, not suitable for pork-free diets.
Lamb skewer & duck liver parfait — both contain pork (pancetta in the skewer, pork in the parfait ingredients). A lamb dish and a duck dish that are not pork-free.
Rigatoni alla vodka — the vodka is not fully cooked out. Worth flagging for guests avoiding alcohol.
Insalata & fungo arrostito — both contain gluten (croutons in the salad, breadcrumb crust on the mushroom). Easy to overlook on a "salad" or a "mushroom."
Duck croquette, saccottini, parfait, tiramisu, affogato — all contain real alcohol (Muscat, red wine, Madeira/port/brandy, Strega/Sambuca, liqueur of choice). Hidden alcohol across the menu.
05

Anaphylaxis: the emergency

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. It can come on within minutes. Everyone on shift should be able to spot it and know the first move — minutes matter.

Our venue procedure — when a guest tells you they are anaphylactic The moment a guest says they have an anaphylactic allergy, do two things straight away, before anything else:
1. Log it on Lightspeed immediately so it's attached to the table/order.   2. Tell the manager.
This makes sure everyone who touches that order — floor, kitchen, pass — knows before a single plate is made.

Know the signs

Mild to moderate — watch closely
  • Swelling of lips, face or eyes
  • Hives, welts or a body rash
  • Tingling or itchy mouth
  • Stomach pain or vomiting
Severe (anaphylaxis) — act now
  • Difficult or noisy breathing
  • Swelling of the tongue / throat tightness
  • Hoarse voice or trouble talking
  • Persistent cough or wheeze
  • Dizziness, collapse, or going pale & floppy

If you see ANY severe sign — act immediately

Lay the person flat. Do not let them stand or walk. If breathing is hard, let them sit — never stand them up suddenly.
Get a manager and our adrenaline auto-injector now. We keep one on site and our managers are trained to give it. If the guest has their own injector or ASCIA Action Plan, use that.
Give the adrenaline into the outer mid-thigh without delay — adrenaline first, always.
Call 000 for an ambulance and say the word "anaphylaxis."
Stay with them. A second dose can be given after 5 minutes if there's no improvement. Symptoms can return hours later (biphasic) — they must go to hospital even if they seem to recover.
Remember Adrenaline is the only treatment for anaphylaxis — and it's safe to give if you're unsure. Antihistamines and asthma puffers do not stop anaphylaxis. When in doubt, give adrenaline and call 000.

We keep an adrenaline auto-injector on site and our managers are trained to administer it — know where it's kept and which managers are on shift. Always also follow any individual guest's ASCIA Action Plan, and call 000.
06

Our service procedure

Knowing the theory means nothing if it breaks down at service. This is exactly how we handle allergies at every table, every time.

Step by step — every table

  1. Ask before the menu. As soon as you greet a table — before handing over or talking through the menu — ask if anyone has a food allergy. Knowing first lets you guide them to the dishes they can and can't have.
  2. If a guest says they're anaphylactic: log it on Lightspeed straight away and tell the manager — before taking the rest of the order.
  3. Not sure if a dish is safe? Don't guess. Check the Allergy Matrix — our plate-by-allergen grid, found on Lightspeed, behind the bar, and at the back-of-house station — to see exactly which allergens each dish contains.
  4. Allergen not listed in the Matrix? Go and ask the chef directly — never assume it's fine.
  5. Entering the order. Always put an allergy order through the Allergy Matrix in the system, so the kitchen receives the verified allergen information with the docket.

And always — the habits that keep it safe

A

Confirm & repeat back

Take every allergy seriously, write it down, and read it back to the guest so nothing is misheard.

B

Stop cross-contamination

Clean surfaces, fresh gloves, separate boards, clean utensils and pans — no shared fryer or shared tongs for an allergy order.

C

Mind the hidden sources

Check sauces, stocks, dressings, oils, garnishes and marinades — that's where allergens hide, not just the obvious ingredient.

D

Never downplay it

Don't say "it should be fine" or "just a little." For an allergic guest, a trace can be life-threatening. Be honest about risk.
