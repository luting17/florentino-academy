01

The Florentino philosophy

The objective is not to sell more food and wine. The objective is to create a better experience — and when the experience is better, spending takes care of itself.

Guests should feel welcomed, guided and cared for. When a recommendation genuinely improves their evening, they spend more naturally — not because we pushed, but because we made the night better. That is the whole game: we are hosts first, and the sales follow.

The rule Never recommend something solely because it is expensive. Recommend it because it adds value to the guest's experience.
In the Café The energy is relaxed but the technique is identical. Pick tonight's hero dish before service — "Save room, the tiramisu goes fast." Suggest a side with every main, and a glass that pairs with the pasta.
02

Understanding guest psychology

People don't buy products — they buy confidence. Yours transfers to them. Guests spend more when these five things are true:

TrustThey trust the server.
UnderstoodThey feel understood.
PersonalThey believe the recommendation is personalised.
OccasionThey feel they are celebrating or treating themselves.
ExpertiseThey see the server as an expert.
Remember People buy confidence more than products. Your confidence transfers to the guest — so speak with warmth and certainty.
03

The complete service sequence

Ten stages, every table. Each one is a chance to enhance the guest experience — not just to take an order.

  1. Greeting
    Warm, genuine, eye contact. Set the tone for the whole evening.
  2. Water first
    "Still or sparkling?" — then the wine list and cocktail list go down.
  3. The drinks order
    You return and take the drinks — a glass, a cocktail, a round. The visit starts flowing.
  4. Discovery questions
    Ask before you recommend — learn the occasion and the mood.
  5. Food menus down
    Only now — drinks are on their way, the food story begins.
  6. The full order
    We aim to take everything at once: starters, mains, sides — and the glass that pairs with the pasta.
  7. Check-backs
    Top up drinks, celebrate the choices — and plant the tiramisu on a high note.
  8. Dessert after mains
    If dessert wasn't taken with the order, return after the mains — harvest what you planted.
  9. Coffee & affogato close
    The easiest yes of the day.
  10. Warm farewell
    End as you began — make them want to return.
04

Discovery questions

Ask before recommending. Good questions build trust and reveal exactly where the opportunities are.

  • "Are you celebrating anything special tonight?"
  • "What are you most excited to enjoy this evening?"
  • "Do you prefer something lighter, or more indulgent?"
  • "Would you like a few recommendations?"
Why it worksThese questions make the guest feel understood — and they hand you everything you need to recommend with precision.
05

Language that sells

The words you choose shape how a guest imagines a dish before it arrives. Swap flat, closed questions for warm, consultative ones — and paint a picture.

✕ Avoid

  • "Would you like anything else?"
  • "Do you want a side?"
  • "That's our most expensive wine."

✓ Use

  • "May I make a recommendation?"
  • "Many guests enjoy…"
  • "This pairs beautifully with…"
  • "The kitchen is particularly proud of…"
  • "If you're looking for something special…"

Be specific

Instead of "Would you like anything else?", ask: "Would you like a coffee to finish — or perhaps an affogato, espresso over gelato?" Specifics sound professional and consultative, and give the guest something concrete to say yes to.

Paint a picture

The words we choose change how a guest perceives a dish. Replace "nice" or "good" with divine, elegant, indulgent, beautifully crafted. "The tiramisu is absolutely divine" is far more persuasive than "It's nice." Great hospitality lets guests imagine and anticipate the experience before the dish even arrives.

Social proof & authority

People trust what others enjoy. These phrases quietly reduce the risk in a guest's mind:

"Many of our regulars enjoy this."
"One of our most requested dishes."
"The chef is particularly proud of this dish."
"Our sommelier loves this pairing."
06

The upsell journey, course by course

Guide the guest through a complete dining journey. Never ask "Do you want…?" — paint a picture instead.

Starters

Goal · build excitement
Arancini: "The gorgonzola arancini — wrapped in Ramarro Farm radicchio — are lovely to share while you decide."
Scallop: "Many guests start with the wood-fired scallop — cooked in the half shell with n'duja and honey butter."

Never ask "Do you want a starter?" — paint a picture instead.

Pasta & mains

Goal · guide the journey · a side for every main
"The ravioli doppio is house-made and one of our most requested dishes."
"Tonight's market fish is elegant and generous — the kitchen is particularly proud of it."
"The cotoletta is a guest favourite — crisp, golden and generous."
Guiding a side: "The wood-fired broccolini or the shaved fennel salad rounds the plate beautifully — which would you prefer?"

Dessert strategy

Goal · plant before mains are finished
"Save room — the tiramisu goes fast, and it's beautifully made. Shall I set one aside?"

Guests order dessert far more often when they're given time to consider it — so plant the idea early.

Wine sales framework

Goal · pair to the experience, not the price

✕ Never ask

  • "Would you like wine?"

✓ Instead ask

  • "May I recommend something that pairs beautifully with your selection?"
With the rigatoni alla vodka: a glass of the Le Corti Chianti Classico. With the cured marlin: a crisp Pinot Grigio.
With the Bonet Piemontese: a glass of Vin Santo to finish.

Focus on style · occasion · pairing · experience — and avoid focusing on price.

07

The four phrases — for shy staff

If you remember nothing else, remember these four. They carry you through any table.

"May I recommend…"
"Many guests enjoy…"
"This pairs beautifully with…"
"The kitchen is particularly proud of…"
The challenge Make at least one genuine recommendation at every table. Confidence comes from repetition.
08

Handling objections

Never argue. Always guide. An objection is just an invitation to offer a better-fitting option.

Guest: "We just want something quick."
"Of course — the pasta comes out fast, and the ravioli doppio is a guest favourite. A glass to go with it?"
Guest: "We're not having starters."
"No problem — the gorgonzola arancini take five minutes and are perfect to share while you decide."
Guest: "That's a bit much."
"Absolutely — another lovely option is the market fish, which is wonderful value tonight."
The principleNever argue. Always guide them toward an alternative that fits.
09

Know your guest

Read the table and adapt. The same warmth, delivered four different ways.

Celebration guests

Lean into champagne, caviar, premium wines and dessert.

Business guests

Be efficient and concise. Respect their time and their conversation.

Food enthusiasts

Tell stories about ingredients and producers — they came for the detail.

Regulars

Personalise from previous visits. Make them feel known.

10

The recommendation follow-up

Checking back after a recommendation shows confidence and genuine interest. When a guest compliments a dish, use it to reinforce the recommendation and deepen rapport.

A simple formula to remember
Recommend→ Check Back→ Celebrate the Choice→ Suggest the Next Experience
This turns a recommendation into a relationship — rather than just a sale.

When a guest tells you they loved it, reinforce your recommendation:

"I'm so glad you enjoyed it — that's actually one of my favourite dishes on the menu, and one of the reasons I recommended it."
"Fantastic — it's a dish I often recommend because it consistently impresses our guests."

If the guest loves the dish

  • "I'm so pleased you enjoyed it."
  • "I had a feeling that would be the perfect choice for you."
  • "That's exactly why it's one of my favourite recommendations."
  • "I'm glad it lived up to expectations."

To build credibility & trust

  • "It's one of our most consistently praised dishes."
  • "Many of our regulars come back specifically for that."
  • "The chef does an incredible job with that dish."
  • "It showcases the kitchen at its best."

To create another opportunity

  • "If you're considering dessert, I have a favourite recommendation there too."
  • "Based on your feedback, I think you'd also enjoy…"
  • "Now that you've enjoyed that course, I'd love to suggest a wine or dessert to complement it."

To create a personal connection

  • "Thank you for trusting my recommendation."
  • "You've chosen one of my favourites."
  • "I was hoping you'd enjoy that one."
  • "It's nice to see it was exactly what you were looking for."
